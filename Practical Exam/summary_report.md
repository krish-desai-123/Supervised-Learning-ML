# Summary Report — Customer Churn Prediction

## Business Problem & Dataset Summary
Telecom providers like Jio or Airtel lose paying subscribers every month, and acquiring a replacement customer (CAC) costs far more than retaining an existing one (CLV). This project used the IBM Telco Customer Churn dataset (7,043 customers, 21 features covering demographics, services, contract, and billing) to build a binary classifier predicting whether a customer will churn, so the retention team can proactively target at-risk subscribers. The target class is imbalanced (~26.5% churn vs ~73.5% retained), and `TotalCharges` required cleaning (11 blank-string rows, all corresponding to brand-new customers with `tenure = 0`).

## Preprocessing & Class Imbalance Strategy
The `customerID` column was dropped (no predictive value), and `TotalCharges` was converted to numeric and filled with the median. Three engineered features were added: `tenure_group` (New/Mid/Senior/Loyal, label-encoded to preserve order), `num_services` (count of subscribed add-ons), and `AutoPay` (binary flag for automatic payment methods). Binary columns were mapped to 0/1, and nominal categoricals (`InternetService`, `Contract`, `PaymentMethod`) were one-hot encoded. Distance-based models (KNN, SVM) required `StandardScaler` on `tenure`, `MonthlyCharges`, `TotalCharges`, and `num_services`; the scaler was fit on the training split only to avoid leakage. An 80/20 stratified train-test split was performed first, and **SMOTE was applied only to the training set** to balance the ~73/27 split to ~50/50, leaving the test set untouched for an honest, real-world evaluation.

## Model Recommendation
Four classifiers were tuned and compared, prioritizing **Recall on the Churn class** as the primary metric — a False Negative (a churner the model misses) costs the company a lost customer entirely, while a False Positive only costs an unnecessary retention offer. Tuned KNN (k selected via F1 sweep) achieved the highest Recall (~0.80), with Naive Bayes close behind (~0.78 Recall) and the best overall AUC-ROC (~0.82) of all four models. Decision Tree (max_depth tuned via cross-validation) and SVM (C tuned via 3-fold CV) trailed on Recall, and SVM's training time (~19s) was orders of magnitude higher than the other three (<0.04s each).

**Recommended model: Naive Bayes**, packaged as the deployed pipeline (`churn_model.pkl`). It offers strong Recall, the best AUC-ROC, near-instant training/scoring suited to a daily batch-scoring job, and simple, transparent probability outputs the retention team can act on directly. KNN is retained as a viable secondary/ensemble candidate given its top Recall, and the Decision Tree is kept specifically for its interpretability — its visualized splits and feature importances explain *why* a customer is flagged, which Naive Bayes' probability scores alone cannot.

## Top 3 Churn Signals & Recommended Actions
1. **Contract type (Month-to-month):** The single strongest churn driver — these customers face no switching penalty. *Action:* target month-to-month customers with incentivized upgrades to 1- or 2-year contracts (loyalty discounts, locked-in pricing).
2. **Tenure (especially under 12 months):** New customers churn far more than long-tenured ones. *Action:* build a structured "first-year" onboarding and check-in program (proactive support outreach at 30/90/180 days) to reduce early attrition.
3. **MonthlyCharges (higher spend correlates with higher churn):** Price-sensitive customers, often on Fiber internet, churn more. *Action:* offer targeted retention discounts or service bundles to high-bill customers before they consider switching providers, and audit Fiber service quality/competitiveness in affected regions.

Error analysis also revealed that missed churners (False Negatives) skew toward **longer-tenured, higher-TotalCharges customers on longer contracts** — an atypical churn profile the model under-flags. This suggests supplementing the model with qualitative signals (support tickets, complaint logs, NPS surveys) specifically for long-tenured customers, since billing/usage data alone doesn't fully predict their churn.

## Next Steps
With more time and resources, the recommended next steps are: (1) **more data** — incorporate customer support interaction logs, NPS/satisfaction survey scores, and competitor pricing data, which would likely improve detection of the "atypical" long-tenure churners identified in error analysis; (2) **deep learning** — only worth exploring if the dataset grows substantially larger, since on this relatively small, structured 7K-row dataset, classical models like Naive Bayes and tuned trees are more appropriate and already perform competitively; and (3) **a real-time scoring API** — wrap the saved `churn_model.pkl` pipeline in a lightweight REST endpoint (e.g., FastAPI) so the retention CRM can pull a live churn-probability score per customer and auto-prioritize the daily call list for the team's ~500/day capacity.
